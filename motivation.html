MOTIVATION

Why HCMC Needs More Than a Metro Project

Ho Chi Minh City is entering a major mobility transition. Metro Line 1 is already operational, Line 2 is under construction, and the city is moving toward smarter, data-driven transport governance. The central question is whether metro development can become the backbone of a broader system transition rather than remain an isolated infrastructure project.

Context
Motorcycle-dominant city
Motorcycles remain the dominant mode of everyday travel, offering door-to-door convenience but contributing to mixed traffic and low road efficiency.
Pressure
Rapid growth and congestion
Urbanization and rising travel demand create severe congestion and safety challenges across the city.
Transition
Emerging metro system
Metro investment creates a window of opportunity to reduce dependence on motorcycles and reorganize urban mobility around public transport corridors.
Governance
Data-driven planning
Smart traffic management and mobility data systems are becoming essential for monitoring, evaluation, and adaptive decision-making.

The Core Mobility Challenge

HCMC is often described as a motorcycle city. Motorcycles account for about 74% of daily trips, while public transport accounts for only a small share of urban travel. This structure is not simply a matter of vehicle preference; it reflects the interaction between travel behavior, street conditions, public transport reliability, land use, and first/last-mile access.

Motorcycles are effective but structurally dominant
They provide flexibility, short-distance speed, and easy parking, but also reinforce mixed traffic conflicts and road-space inefficiency.
Public transport is not yet competitive enough
Long or uncertain waiting time, limited passenger information, inadequate stop conditions, and first/last-mile gaps reduce willingness to shift modes.
Metro alone cannot reshape the system
Field observations and corridor comparison suggest that metro operation can bring localized improvement, but broader modal change requires coordinated support systems.

Existing Modal Structure

The baseline modal structure shows why the transition challenge is not simply about adding infrastructure, but about shifting the conditions that make motorcycles the default choice.

Research Question

How can HCMC use metro development and smart mobility planning to drive a broader transition toward a more integrated and sustainable urban transport system?

From Infrastructure to System Transition

The project starts from a simple premise: improving mobility is not only about building more transport infrastructure. It requires understanding how demand, supply, accessibility, walkability, and user behavior interact across space and time.

From Data Gap to Integrated Evidence

Because high-resolution, mode-specific traffic evidence is limited, the analysis combines street-view detection, road network indicators, public transport schedules, accessibility modelling, and field observation under a shared grid-based framework.

From Analysis to Planning Action

The final goal is to translate mobility evidence into strategy: improving public transport operations, station access, first/last-mile connections, traffic management, and data-driven governance.
