CONCLUSION

From Metro Project to System Transition

This project asked how Ho Chi Minh City can use metro development and smart mobility planning to support a broader transition toward a more integrated and sustainable urban transport system. The answer is not a single infrastructure solution, but a coordinated system transition built around public transport, walkability, first/last-mile access, traffic management, and data-driven governance.

Metro alone cannot transform urban mobility.

Metro investment provides an important backbone, but the evidence shows that its impact remains limited if it is not supported by reliable bus operations, safe pedestrian access, first/last-mile services, and adaptive traffic management. The goal is not to immediately remove motorcycles, but to make alternative modes reliable enough for a gradual and realistic modal transition.

Answer to the research question

HCMC can use metro development as a catalyst, not as a standalone solution. Metro corridors should become anchors for integrated mobility reform: bus–metro coordination, station-area walkability, shared mobility nodes, mixed traffic management, and real-time data feedback.

The shift should therefore be framed as rebalancing urban mobility: reducing unnecessary road-space pressure, improving public transport attractiveness, and gradually changing travel behavior through coordinated system design.

1. Key Findings

The analysis shows that HCMC's mobility challenge is structural rather than temporary. Motorcycles dominate because they provide flexible, door-to-door, short-distance mobility in a city where public transport reliability, walking conditions, transfer quality, and first/last-mile access remain uneven.

01
Metro effects are visible but localized
The comparison between Line 1 and Line 2 shows early signs of traffic redistribution and congestion relief near operational metro corridors, but these effects are not strong enough to alter the wider motorcycle-dominant mobility structure.
02
Motorcycle dependence is system-based
Motorcycle use remains attractive because it solves practical gaps left by the current system: uncertain waiting times, weak feeder connections, inadequate stop conditions, limited passenger information, and poor station access.
03
Transit supply alone has limited effect
Scenario testing confirms that increasing transit supply alone produces only marginal modal shift. Stronger impacts require coordinated improvements in walkability, accessibility, land-use integration, and TOD-oriented development.
04
Congestion reflects multi-factor mismatch
Congestion is not simply high traffic volume. It emerges from the interaction between demand pressure, road supply, transit accessibility, land use, motorcycle intensity, and time-specific movement patterns.

2. What This Means for HCMC

The central planning implication is that mobility improvement should move beyond the idea of building isolated transport infrastructure. HCMC's challenge is to convert metro corridor potential into a functioning mobility system that people can actually use in everyday travel.

This means improving the whole chain of movement: getting to stations, transferring between modes, waiting comfortably, receiving reliable service information, and moving safely through mixed-traffic streets. Without these supporting systems, metro development will remain an infrastructure upgrade rather than a full mobility transition.

3. Strategic Direction

The proposed strategy is not to replace motorcycles immediately. A more realistic approach is to use metro expansion as the backbone of a gradual transition, supported by four complementary strategy loops.

Bus / Metro Operation
Increase service frequency, improve reliability, support dynamic dispatch, and coordinate bus feeder routes with metro corridors.
Walkability and Station Access
Improve sidewalks, safe crossings, shaded routes, station-area walking zones, and barrier-free access for daily users.
First / Last Mile Connection
Organize shared electric motorcycles, micro-mobility hubs, feeder services, and integrated mobility systems around transit nodes.
Traffic Management
Manage mixed traffic through motorcycle lane organization, intersection management, one-way circulation, and adaptive signal control.

4. From Strategy to Adaptive Governance

The project also demonstrates that mobility planning should not stop at static recommendations. Because demand, congestion, and transport performance vary across space and time, HCMC needs a data-driven platform that can continuously monitor, diagnose, evaluate, and update interventions.

The Mobility Analyzer developed in this project points toward this future. It connects grid-level indicators, congestion mechanisms, land-use context, street-view evidence, and AI-assisted interpretation into a planning interface. This turns mobility analysis from a one-time study into a repeatable decision-support workflow for transport agencies and urban planners.

5. Path Toward Implementation

The transition should be phased. HCMC does not need to redesign the entire mobility system at once. A more realistic pathway is to begin with corridor-level pilots, then scale toward network coordination and citywide smart mobility governance.

Phase 1 · 2026–2030
Metro Line 2 Pilot Integration
Focus on Line 2 and high-demand station areas. Test bus coordination, station access upgrades, walkability improvements, and first/last-mile services.
Phase 2 · 2028–2040
Network Expansion and Coordination
Extend strategies to multiple metro corridors, optimize bus networks and interchanges, and strengthen coordination between metro, bus, motorcycle, and traffic systems.
Phase 3 · 2035–2050
Citywide Transformation
Move toward full mobility integration through AI-supported planning, real-time data governance, TOD development, land-use restructuring, and long-term urban regeneration.

Final Message

HCMC's mobility future should not be framed as a simple replacement of motorcycles by metro. It should be understood as a broader rebalancing process: strengthening public transport attractiveness, reducing road-space pressure, improving access to stations, organizing mixed traffic, and using data feedback to continuously refine decisions.

The most important opportunity is therefore not only the construction of new metro lines, but the creation of a coordinated mobility system around them.
