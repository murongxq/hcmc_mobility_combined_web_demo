PART I · DESKTOP ANALYSIS

Traffic Distribution

This part examines how different transport modes appear across Ho Chi Minh City using street-view traffic detection. It does not treat mobility as a single traffic volume problem; instead, it separates motorcycles, cars, and buses to reveal the spatial structure of a motorcycle-dominant urban system.

Why this layer matters

Ho Chi Minh City is widely characterized as a “motorcycle city”. Motorcycles account for the largest share of daily travel, while public transport remains marginal in the current modal structure. Part I therefore starts from observed street-level evidence: where each mode is visible, where it is concentrated, and where local traffic composition deviates from the citywide baseline.

Analytical flow

1Detect motorcycles, cars, and buses from street-view images.
2Aggregate detections into a unified 250m grid to support citywide comparison.
3Compare count, mean intensity, observed mode share, and traffic composition typology.

1) Street-View Detection by Mode

This first layer maps where motorcycles, cars, and buses are captured in street-view images. It provides the observational base for understanding spatial exposure before moving into normalized grid indicators.

Data source and object classes

  • Street-view images with timestamp and location metadata
  • Detected object classes: motorcycle, car, and bus
  • Grid-level aggregation used to reduce point-level noise

How to read this map

  • Count indicates total detected vehicles aggregated into each 250m grid cell.
  • Higher values indicate stronger street-level exposure captured by available SVI samples.
  • This layer should be interpreted with mean intensity because count is also affected by image sampling density.
COUNT · MOTORCYCLE
Field: n_motorcycle
Detection count identifies where each mode is frequently observed, but it is not a direct travel-volume estimate. It is best used as the first descriptive layer in the traffic distribution analysis.

2) Grid-Based Mean Traffic Intensity

This step moves from raw detections to typical mode-specific intensity. By averaging observations within each grid and time unit, the analysis better captures persistent traffic presence rather than isolated image-level spikes.

Step 1 — Hourly grid mean

Ig,h,d(m) = (1 / Ng,h,d) × Σi ∈ (g,h,d) ni(m)
where g = grid, h = hour, d = weekday/weekend, m = mode, and n is detected count per image.

Step 2 — all-time mean per grid

Īg(m) = (1 / Tg) × Σh,d Ig,h,d(m)
This produces one comparable intensity value per grid and mode.

Interpretation

  • Mean intensity highlights persistent mode-specific hotspots.
  • It supports comparison between motorcycles, cars, and buses under the same spatial unit.
  • For HCMC, this is especially useful for distinguishing motorcycle-dominant areas from car-oriented or bus-visible corridors.
MEAN · MOTORCYCLE
Field: n_motorcycle_mean_all
This map supports the first major analytical goal of the project: identifying mode-specific vehicle intensity across space.

3) Mode Composition: Observed Share

Observed mode share shifts the focus from total intensity to relative composition. This helps identify where local street exposure is disproportionately shaped by motorcycles, cars, or buses.

Definition

Share = mode mean / total mean Sg(m) = Īg(m) / Σk Īg(k)
where k ∈ {motorcycle, car, bus}.

What it represents

  • Relative composition within each grid cell
  • Street-level exposure structure, not household travel behavior
  • Not equivalent to travel survey modal split
SHARE · MOTORCYCLE
Field: share_motorcycle_all
Mode share separates “busy places” from “mode-dominant places”. This is important in HCMC because motorcycle dominance can be structural even where total detected traffic is not the highest.

4) Traffic Composition Typology

The final layer translates observed composition into interpretable traffic types by measuring each grid’s deviation from the citywide baseline.

Classification logic

Step 1 — Baseline: citywide average share by mode Step 2 — Deviation: local share − city baseline Step 3 — Classification: top 20% deviation
This follows the PDF logic: baseline, deviation, and top-20% classification.

Traffic types

  • Motorcycle Baseline
  • Car-Oriented
  • Bus-Influenced
  • Mixed
  • Low Confidence

These categories summarize how local traffic composition differs from the HCMC-wide pattern.

Takeaway from Part I

Part I establishes the baseline spatial evidence for HCMC’s mobility structure. It shows not only where traffic is concentrated, but also how the balance between motorcycles, cars, and buses varies across the city. This provides the first analytical foundation for Part II, where traffic pressure is compared with road supply to diagnose congestion mechanisms.
